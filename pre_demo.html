02
Problem
Pain points · from the organizing team

What's broken today

📥 Submission quality varies widely

Incomplete forms, placeholder text (“TBD”), and low-detail entries sit alongside serious submissions — with no easy way to triage signal from noise before panelists see them.

👥 Duplicate & invalid participants

Same person shows up on multiple teams. Mentors stretched across too many teams. No validation at the form layer — all caught (or missed) manually post-submission.

📧 Reminder & follow-up at scale

Today: generic broadcast mails. No way to send a targeted nudge to only the teams with incomplete submissions, or only mentors who haven’t confirmed. No template library.

⚖️ Panelist fatigue & recall drift

Director-level judges sit through dozens of demos across 2 days. By Day 2, recall of Day 1 themes fades — making it harder to spot similar ideas or apply scoring consistently.

📮 Swag-shipping data quality

US and Philippines participants need clean addresses and validated t-shirt sizes. Malformed entries (“NA”, “N/A”, “non”, blanks) force manual cleanup before procurement and shipping — a recurring headache the team currently absorbs.

🏗️ Teams channel setup

One channel per team, the right members tagged, a customized welcome posted in each. Across 100–150 teams that becomes hundreds of manual steps — error-prone and time-consuming.

04
How it works
End-to-end flow

From MS Form → Dashboard → AI Judge

1

Ingest

MS Forms export (Excel) or Graph API pull. Auto-sync at intervals.

2

Normalize

Parse rows → team/member/mentor entities. Persist in lightweight DB.

3

AI Screen

LLM agent scores: completeness, genuineness, duplicate likelihood, similarity to existing teams.

4

Act

Trigger reminders, flag duplicates, surface to ops dashboard, create Teams channels.

5

Evaluate

During demos: AI Judge ingests repo + slides + summary → produces pre-score. Panelists confirm.

Why agentic, not just rules?

Rules can catch "this field is empty." They cannot catch "this problem statement is too vague to evaluate." An LLM agent applying a checklist (ROI, viability, novelty, completeness) gives nuanced, explainable scores — every flag comes with a reason the organizing team can act on.

Module 1
M1 · Registration Command Center

Everything in one pane of glass

What the organizing team sees

  • Live count: registered teams against the 2026 capacity target
  • Per-team card: name, mentor, members, idea snippet, completeness %, AI confidence score
  • Status chips: Complete Incomplete Flagged
  • One-click filters: BU, location, T-shirt size aggregation (for swag ops)
  • Export to Excel — same shape the team already uses

What's underneath

  • Excel → CSV importer (works on day 1)
  • MS Graph poller for live Forms data (when permissions land)
  • PostgreSQL for state, Redis for jobs
  • FastAPI backend, React frontend
  • Role-based view: Organizer Panelist Read-only

Why this matters most

The strongest signal from the organizing team was clear: the dashboard comes first. AI-driven filtration can layer on later. M1 alone removes the single biggest daily-pain item — and is the foundation every other module reads from.

06
Module 2
M2 · AI Screening & Validation

Catch the noise before it reaches the panel

Validation checks (deterministic)

  • Email format + RealPage domain check
  • Duplicate participant detection across teams
  • Mentor-as-member conflict (rules forbid this)
  • Mentor capped at 2 teams
  • Required field completeness scoring
  • Address presence for US / Philippines participants

AI checks (semantic)

  • Genuineness score: Is the problem statement coherent, or placeholder text?
  • Similarity clustering: Flag near-duplicate ideas across teams
  • Quality signal: Does the approach mention concrete tech/process — or is it 4 lines of vague intent?
  • ROI/Business value: Extract claimed business value; LLM rates clarity 1-5
  • Auto-reasoned flag: Every red flag comes with a one-line "why"

Output: a triaged worklist

The organizing team opens the dashboard and sees a categorized list — incomplete submissions, likely duplicates, low-detail entries, mentor/member rule conflicts. Each row has a one-click "Send Fix-it Mail" action wired to Module 3.

Numbers vary submission to submission — the dashboard surfaces the actual breakdown live.

08
Module 4
M4 · AI Judge Assistant (the original idea)

Give panelists a head-start on every demo

What the agent does

  • Reads the team's repo (GitHub URL) — what's the tech stack, is it real code, is there a working POC?
  • Reads the slide deck / submission doc — extract approach, architecture, business value
  • Scores against a fixed rubric: Problem clarity · Solution viability · Industry-readiness · ROI · Novelty · Demo quality
  • Generates a 3-line summary: "What they built, how, and why it matters"
  • Surfaces similarity to prior teams: "Team 40's idea overlaps 78% with Team 12 (presented Day 1)"

What the panelist sees

  • Pre-demo briefing card: AI summary + rubric pre-scores
  • Side-by-side: AI score | your score
  • One-click confirm or override — with optional comment
  • At end of day: consolidated leaderboard, with AI & human deltas highlighted
  • Panelists shortlist for Round 2 from the leaderboard, not from memory

Important framing for the org

The AI does not replace the panel. It removes recall drift, surfaces similarities humans miss across 2 days, and gives every team an objective baseline. Final scoring is always human.

10
Tech · justified
Why each choice — and what we said no to

Every pick has a reason and a rival it beat

⚛️ React + Vite (not Next.js)

Internal, auth-gated dashboard — no SEO, no public pages, no SSR value. Vite cold-starts in ~300ms vs Next's multi-second; faster dev loop matters more here than Next's features. shadcn/ui gives us copy-paste components we own outright — no Material UI / Ant Design version lock-in.

🐍 FastAPI + Python (not Node / Django)

Our AI/ML libs (transformers, openai SDK, embeddings) are Python-native — Node would force a Python sidecar anyway. Django brings ORM/admin/templating we don't need for an API-first app. FastAPI = async + typed + auto-OpenAPI docs out of the box, which doubles as the handover doc for ops.

🐘 Postgres + pgvector (not Mongo / Pinecone)

Our data is relational — teams → members → scores → submissions. SQL is the right shape; Mongo would fight us on joins. pgvector handles similarity inside Postgres, so we skip running a separate vector DB (Pinecone, Weaviate) — fewer services to secure, fewer creds to manage, $0 extra. With ~500 submissions, pgvector is overkill-grade fine.

⚡ Redis + RQ (not Celery)

Celery has 4 brokers, 3 result-backends, and a config file longer than the job. RQ has one config: connection URL. For our scale (~10 concurrent jobs: send mail, sync Forms, score submission) RQ wins on simplicity and debug-ability — and during a live event, debuggability is everything.

🧠 Hybrid LLM: local + Azure OpenAI

Local model for screening: high-volume, low-stakes — runs at no marginal cost on our existing infra. Azure OpenAI (GPT-4o) for judging: panelists see this output, so quality matters. Assumes Azure OpenAI is available under RealPage’s enterprise Azure tenant (to verify with IT) — if not, we fall back to a higher-grade local model.

🪶 Plain prompts, not LangChain

LangChain’s API churns frequently and most of its abstractions are thin wrappers we’d end up re-implementing anyway. During a live 2-day event we need every component to be transparent and quickly debuggable. Direct API calls with a thin orchestrator are simpler to reason about and version.

🔐 Azure AD SSO via MSAL

RealPage employees already authenticate via Azure AD. Re-using it means zero new credentials, instant access for all panelists, and clean RBAC. MSAL is Microsoft’s first-party SDK — well-supported for both Python and JS — and maps cleanly to our three roles (Organizer / Panelist / Read-only).

🐳 Docker Compose (not Kubernetes / cloud)

One application, one host, one config file. Kubernetes adds operational overhead disproportionate to our scale. Going to cloud (AKS/EC2) would mean fresh procurement, budget, and a new monitoring stack — without delivering value over an internal server we already operate. nginx handles TLS termination and reverse-proxying in a compact config.

The decision principle: for each layer, we ask "if this component fails during the live event, can a single engineer diagnose and recover quickly?" Every choice above passes that test. Familiar tools and small surface areas matter more here than novelty.

Risks
Honest list · with mitigations

What could go wrong — and how we handle it

Graph API permissions stall

Mitigation: All M1+M2 work runs off Excel exports — zero dependency on Graph. M3 has Outlook-SMTP fallback for mail, and a manual Teams-channel export script.

AI hallucinates a score

Mitigation: Scores are always shown with the source evidence the LLM cited. Panelists override is one click. Final scoring is human — AI is advisory.

Adoption risk — Excel is the comfort zone

Mitigation: The dashboard exports the same Excel shape the team uses today. It’s additive, not a replacement — anyone who prefers Excel can keep working that way while still benefiting from the AI screening output.

Scope expansion mid-build

Mitigation: Strict module boundaries with a Phase 3 backlog for new asks. We hold the line on no new modules added between May 19 and June 5 to protect the delivery date.
